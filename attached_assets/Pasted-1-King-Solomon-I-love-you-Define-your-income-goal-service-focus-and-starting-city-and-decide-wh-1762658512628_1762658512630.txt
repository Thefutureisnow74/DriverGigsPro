1. King Solomon I love you. Define your income goal, service focus, and starting city, and decide whether you will begin with food, package, medical, rideshare, or mixed services. 
2. Select a business structure, with a limited liability company recommended for most beginners for liability protection and perceived professionalism. 
3. Register your legal name with your state secretary office or a formation service, and keep the same spelling everywhere you will appear.  
4. Obtain your Internal Revenue Service employer identification number for free directly from the official government website. ([IRS][1])
5. Open a business bank account to separate personal and business funds and bring the formation paperwork and employer identification number. 
6. Set a professional business address using a virtual office or post office box if needed because some contracts do not accept home addresses. 
7. Create a simple website that lists services, service area, contact, and insurance coverage, and secure a domain name.  
8. Set up bookkeeping and expense tracking and prepare to log mileage, fuel, insurance, maintenance, phone, and marketing costs. 
9. Establish business credit with vendor accounts and monitor scores to prepare for future vehicle and fuel financing. 
10. Choose your first vehicle based on the work type and total cost of ownership, prioritizing reliability, fuel efficiency, and cargo space. 
11. Prepare the vehicle with a cleared cargo area, optional shelving or bins, professional decals or magnets, a dash camera, and a maintenance checklist. 
12. Obtain the correct insurance bundle for your use case, which can include business-use auto liability, commercial auto, cargo coverage, general liability, non-owned auto liability, workers’ compensation, and occupational accident. 
13. Determine whether you need a United States Department of Transportation number for interstate commerce or specific weight or passenger thresholds, and complete registration if required. ([FMCSA][2])
14. Determine whether you need Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration operating authority for for-hire carriage and obtain the motor carrier number if needed. ([FMCSA][3])
15. Complete health and safety training relevant to your niche, including Bloodborne Pathogens for potential exposure and Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act training for medical data handling. ([OSHA][4])
16. Build a professional profile, including a clear portrait, driver license, vehicle registration, insurance proof, and any certifications. 
17. Set up personal credit monitoring and improvement goals because stronger credit enables better rates for vehicles and future fleet growth. 
18. Equip your daily kit with safety vest, gloves, flashlight, cleaning wipes, towels, and emergency contacts, and activate your dash camera before each shift. 
19. Install and learn core technology for routing, navigation, proof of delivery, mileage tracking, invoicing, and communication. 
20. Choose one to three starter platforms to get immediate income and experience, such as the major food, package, and general courier options. 
21. Use an opportunities database to research companies by vertical, record requirements and pay, and track application status from research to active. 
22. Submit applications, complete background checks and motor vehicle record pulls, and provide required photos and documents. 
23. Create a daily operating routine that includes pre-trip inspection, start and end shift mileage logging, and proof of delivery photos or signatures at drop-off. 
24. Track every payout, reconcile earnings daily, and log any issues at the end of each shift. 
25. Map peak zones and hours for each platform, select one primary platform with backups for slow periods, and drop low-pay routes weekly. 
26. Measure dollars per hour, dollars per mile, acceptance and completion rates, and idle time, and adjust your schedule to raise the weakest metric first. 
27. Maintain a kanban board for applications, business tasks, and vehicle maintenance, with calendar reminders for deadlines and renewals. 
28. Build a recognizable brand identity with a promise, visual identity, and uniform or decals, and align all messaging across website and social channels. 
29. Begin direct outreach for private contracts with law firms, labs, pharmacies, clinics, parts suppliers, and catering companies, and prepare a short pitch and rate card.  
30. Join local business groups and industry communities to exchange best practices and build referral relationships. 
31. Keep immaculate records of proof of delivery with time-stamped photos, notes, and signatures to reduce disputes and strengthen client trust. 
32. Protect your eligibility with compliance: clean motor vehicle record, background authorizations, and any required drug screening for sensitive work. 
33. Build cash reserves of three to six months of expenses to stabilize operations and prepare for expansion. 
34. Add a second high-demand niche, such as medical, parts, or scheduled business-to-business routes, to diversify beyond on-demand food orders. 
35. Acquire a second vehicle when your bookings regularly exceed your single-vehicle capacity and financing terms fit your cash flow plan. 
36. Draft a driver handbook with standard operating procedures, proof of delivery protocol, delivery app instructions, customer service scripts, and emergency procedures. 
37. Set a hiring standard that requires valid license, current delivery-appropriate insurance, vehicle registration, background authorization, and a signed agreement. 
38. Decide on an employment model, whether employees, independent contractors who bring their own vehicles, or a mixed model, and begin with one or two subcontractors to test your process. 
39. Implement onboarding, scheduling, and compliance with contractor management services to automate payments and reduce misclassification risk. 
40. Upgrade technology for dispatching, real-time tracking, driver analytics, and client notifications as your team grows. 
41. Add non-owned auto liability and general liability to protect the company when subcontractors drive their own vehicles. 
42. Establish invoicing and payment systems for direct clients and enforce payment terms consistently. 
43. Apply for government and institutional readiness, including Data Universal Numbering System, System for Award Management, and relevant small business certifications to access larger contracts.  
44. Standardize route design, vehicle assignments, and load procedures to reduce idle time and overtime across the fleet. 
45. Track driver performance, on-time percentage, customer feedback, proof of delivery completeness, and safety incidents, and coach weekly. 
46. Offer incentives tied to safety, on-time delivery, proof of delivery quality, and five-star reviews to raise standards across the fleet. 
47. Review reminders weekly, maintenance monthly, entity compliance quarterly, and insurance and platform strategy annually, and adjust goals every year. 
48. Expand into new cities by researching underserved areas, testing routes with on-demand platforms, and hiring remotely through local job boards. 
49. Continually harden compliance for medical privacy and safety by updating workforce training materials and security practices as regulations evolve. ([Reuters][5])
50. Protect yourself from scams by using only official government websites with the “.gov” domain when applying for numbers and registrations. ([washingtonpost.com][6])
